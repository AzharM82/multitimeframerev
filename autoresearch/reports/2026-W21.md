# autoresearch 2026-W21

## TL;DR

Filter out alerts where `reversalPrice > $150`. Expected P&L per signal on
the held-out val split rises from **+$10.99 → +$11.88** (+$0.88, +8.0%);
train moves in the same direction (+$7.80 → +$9.27). Trade count drops by
~40% — the strategy trades less, but each trade has better unit economics.

## Hypothesis

Higher-priced tickers ($200+) need bigger absolute moves to clear the 3%
target. In the baseline data, EOD exits dominate (54% of train, 37% of
val) — many trades drift to close without reaching either TP or SL. The
suspicion: high-price stocks contribute disproportionately to those
drifters. Capping the entry price should preferentially drop the drifty
slice and leave the bar-to-bar reactive names that actually hit targets.

## Side effects

- **Absolute total P&L on val *drops*** from $902 → $570. The strategy
  is more *capital-efficient* per trade but covers less ground. If your
  objective is total realized $ on a fixed $1k-per-trade notional, this
  is a worse outcome ($570 vs $902). If your objective is risk-adjusted
  per-signal expected value (the optimized metric), this is better.
- Coverage: 34 of 82 val signals (41%) are filtered out. The dropped
  set includes alerts on TSLA, INTC, ALAB, MU, ARM, COHR, NBIS — names
  that frequently fired during the val window.
- Both train and val move the same direction, which is the strongest
  reassurance against overfitting given the small val set (n=48 trades).

## What to try next round

Two threads worth probing:

1. **Total-P&L objective.** The price-cap finding optimizes
   expected-value-per-signal but reduces total dollars made. If the user
   trades roughly $1k notional per signal and runs many in parallel,
   *total* P&L over the day matters more than per-trade P&L. A
   reformulated metric (e.g., `total_pnl_val / num_session_days`) might
   pick a different winner — one that keeps the high-price drifters as
   they at least don't lose money on average.

2. **Why are high-price names worse?** TP-hit rates were similar across
   price buckets (~45%), so the issue isn't that high-price names hit
   TP less often — it's that when they don't TP, their drift to EOD is
   smaller as a *percentage* of entry, dragging expected P&L down. A
   *price-relative target* (e.g., TP = max(3%, $5 above entry)) might
   preserve the bigger-stock signals while still capturing the small-cap
   wins. Worth a round of TP-sizing experiments.
